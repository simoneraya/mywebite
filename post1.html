Different Voice, Same Words: 1924 versus 2024

July 13, 2024

Rapid social change was a hallmark of the early 20th century. With the acceleration of industrialization, the demand for cheap and abundant labor surged, leading to a significant increase in immigration. The influx of European immigrants was often blamed by white Americans for the economic hardships and social issues of the era. Simultaneously, the Great Migration saw Black Americans move from the South to Northern regions, further diversifying the demographic landscape of the North and intensifying anti-Black racism and white ethno-nationalist sentiments.

Immigration has long been a focal point of intense political debate in the United States. The perceived threat to a homogenous white America fueled conservative political backlash and a retreat from Wilsonian progressivism. Accordingly, a rise in political attacks on diversity and multiculturalism began to rise, exemplified by the immigration restriction acts of the 1920s. These policies - rooted in eugenics and pseudoscience, aimed to maintain a racially homogenous population by enacting deliberately racist legislation.

The Immigration Act of 1924 epitomized this by codifying racial hierarchies and formalizing a conception of whiteness that dictated social acceptance. The Act introduced the National Origins quota system, which was explicitly race-based, reinforcing an assimilationist structure within American society. This legislation confirmed the United States as a homogenous melting pot rather than a mosaic of cultural identities, creating a new concept of incongruous citizenship for diverse groups excluded from social inclusion.

Fast forward exactly 100 years later to the 2024 presidential election, and we observe a resurgence of similar themes. The rhetoric and political strategies bear a striking resemblance to those of the 1920s. Today, the rapid social changes and demographic shifts continue to be perceived as threats to the traditional, Christian white American identity. The thirst for a conservative, Christian, singular America was never quenched, persisting through decades of social transformation. This perception has given rise to a wave of conservative ascendancy and a revival of anti-immigration and right-wing ethno-nationalist ideologies, echoing the sentiments of the past.

Just as in the 1920s, the current political climate is marked by a resistance to diversity and an emphasis on maintaining a racially and linguistically homogeneous society. The parallels are clear: both eras feature a dominant group feeling besieged by demographic changes and responding with policies and rhetoric aimed at preserving their cultural and racial dominance. The voices advocating for these measures may use different rhetoric today, but the underlying message remains unchanged.
