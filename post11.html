The creator economy likes to describe itself as a rupture.

Legacy media was hierarchical, credentialed, slow. Podcasting is framed as horizontal, accessible, personality-driven. The microphone moves from institutions to individuals. Audiences choose hosts they trust rather than brands they inherit.

At places like Flight Studio, that narrative is often implicit: power has shifted. Media is no longer filtered through elite gatekeepers; it is built around creators and communities.

But the politics of guest booking complicates this story.

Every production team knows the informal list. The “reliable” experts. The people who convert, who clip well, who speak in tight, monetisable soundbites. Names like Andrew Bustamante, Annie Jacobsen, Chase Hughes, Rangan Chatterjee, or Jesse Inchauspé circulate across shows. They become “the” voice on intelligence, geopolitics, behavioural psychology, health, glucose.

This is not necessarily a conspiracy. It is an incentive structure.

Guest booking is shaped by risk management. A repeat guest is predictable. They understand pacing, they respect embargoes, they generate clicks. Booking them lowers uncertainty. For producers working against tight deadlines and performance metrics, that matters.

But the effect is subtle recentralisation.

Authority becomes platform-dependent rather than institution-dependent. Instead of the New York Times columnist or the tenured professor, we get the podcast-native expert. Their legitimacy comes less from peer communities and more from audience familiarity and algorithmic circulation.

The creator economy promises democratisation. Yet attention still concentrates. It is simply redistributed along new lines: charisma, network density, platform literacy.

There is also a feedback loop. Repetition confers expertise. If a guest appears on twenty major podcasts, they begin to feel authoritative irrespective of the depth of their underlying scholarship. Podcasting does not invent expertise out of nothing—but it can amplify thin credentials into thick reputation.

The tension, then, is not between elite and people. It is between different mechanisms of gatekeeping.

Legacy media filtered through editorial institutions. Creator-led media filters through booking teams, audience analytics, and brand alignment. Both systems select. Both systems exclude.

The question is not whether podcasting has democratised media. In many ways, it has. The question is whether it has diversified epistemic authority—or merely rebranded it.
